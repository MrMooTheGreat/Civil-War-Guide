Health & Hygiene

For many soldiers, the greatest threat was not the battlefield, but disease, infection, and poor living conditions in camp.

Disease in the Camps

Disease killed more soldiers than combat during the war. Crowded camps, lack of sanitation, and limited medical knowledge created the perfect conditions for illness to spread rapidly.

  • Dysentery and severe diarrhea
  • Typhoid fever from contaminated water
  • Pneumonia and respiratory infections
  • Malaria in certain regions
Field Hospitals

Overcrowded and under-equipped, hospitals struggled to treat the sick and wounded.

Causes of Illness

Many illnesses were caused by unsanitary conditions in military camps. Waste disposal was often poorly managed, and clean water was not always available.

  • Poor sanitation practices
  • Improper waste disposal
  • Contaminated food supplies
  • Unclean cooking conditions

Soldiers also lacked basic knowledge about hygiene. Germ theory was not widely understood at the time, meaning preventative measures were limited.

  • Infrequent handwashing
  • Shared living quarters
  • Close contact between soldiers
  • Lack of proper bathing facilities
Medical Treatment

Doctors worked with limited tools and knowledge to treat injuries and disease.

Medical Treatment

Medical care during the war was basic and often ineffective. Doctors had limited training, and supplies were frequently scarce.

Common treatments included the use of rudimentary medicines, surgical procedures without modern anesthesia, and basic wound care.

  • Use of crude surgical tools
  • Limited availability of anesthesia
  • High risk of infection after surgery
  • Reliance on trial-and-error treatments

Efforts to Improve Conditions

As the war progressed, some efforts were made to improve sanitation and medical care. Camp layouts were adjusted, and better hygiene practices were gradually encouraged.

  • Designated latrine areas
  • Improved camp organization
  • Increased attention to clean water sources
  • Better ventilation in hospitals

A Hidden Danger

While battles defined the war, disease silently claimed far more lives. The challenges of health and hygiene reveal the harsh realities of daily life for soldiers beyond the battlefield.

These conditions highlight how far modern medicine and sanitation have advanced since that time.
