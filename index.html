Welcome to the army

You’ve traded farm, shop, or city street for a musket and a uniform. Whether you wear Union blue or Confederate gray, your life is about to become a mix of boredom, hardship, fear, and fierce loyalty to the men beside you.

Most of your time will not be spent in glorious charges, but in marching, drilling, waiting, and trying to stay healthy. This guide walks you through what to expect in training, what you’ll wear and carry, how to care for your body, what battle is really like, and how you’ll live in camp.

Training and daily routine

Learning to be a soldier

Training camps turn volunteers into soldiers. Expect long days of drill—learning to march in step, form lines, load and fire your weapon by command, and move as a unit rather than as individuals.

  • Morning: Reveille at dawn, roll call, breakfast, and first drill.
  • Daytime: Company and battalion drill, weapons cleaning, fatigue duties (chopping wood, hauling water, building fortifications).
  • Evening: Dress parade, mail call, perhaps another drill, then taps around 9–9:30 p.m.

Discipline and expectations

  • Obedience: Orders from officers and sergeants are to be followed without argument.
  • Punctuality: Being late to roll call or formation can mean extra duty or punishment.
  • Cleanliness of arms: Your musket or rifle must be kept clean and in working order.
Drill, drill, drill

Only a small fraction of your time will be spent in actual combat—most days are repetitive training and camp duties.

Uniforms and equipment

What you’ll wear

  • Union: Dark blue wool coat or sack coat, light blue trousers, forage cap or kepi, brogans (shoes).
  • Confederate: Gray or “butternut” (brownish) jackets and trousers, often irregular and worn due to supply shortages.
  • Undergarments: Cotton or wool shirts and drawers—often not changed as often as they should be.

What you’ll carry

  • Weapon: Musket or rifle-musket with bayonet; some units carry rifles, carbines, or pistols.
  • Accoutrements: Cartridge box, cap box, belt, bayonet scabbard.
  • Pack: Blanket, extra shirt and socks (if you have them), tin plate and cup, utensils, personal items.
  • Other: Canteen, haversack for rations, sometimes a small Bible, letters, or keepsakes from home.
Blue and gray

Uniforms help identify friend from foe, but in the smoke and confusion of battle, colors and flags are still easy to mistake.

Health and hygiene

Important: In this war, disease kills more soldiers than bullets. Your greatest enemy may be sickness, not the opposing army.

Common dangers

  • Disease: Dysentery, typhoid, pneumonia, measles, and malaria are widespread in crowded camps.
  • Poor sanitation: Contaminated water, badly placed latrines, and rotting garbage spread illness quickly.
  • Malnutrition: Limited diets and spoiled food weaken your body’s ability to fight disease.

What you can do

  • Wash when you can: Clean your hands and face, and change shirts and socks whenever you have the chance.
  • Protect your feet: Keep feet dry, change socks, and treat blisters early to avoid infection.
  • Use latrines properly: Avoid relieving yourself near tents or water sources.
  • Boil water if possible: It can reduce the risk of disease, especially in questionable campsites.

Medical care

Army surgeons work under harsh conditions with limited knowledge of germs. Amputations are common for serious limb wounds, and infection is a constant threat. Hospitals can save lives, but they can also be places where disease spreads.

Field hospitals

Medical care is improving but still primitive. Surviving a wound often depends on luck, cleanliness, and your overall health.

Battles and combat

What battle is really like

You may imagine glory and quick victory. In reality, battle is noise, smoke, confusion, and fear. You’ll stand in long lines, shoulder to shoulder, firing volleys at men doing the same to you. Artillery shells burst overhead; cavalry may threaten your flanks.

  • Before battle: Long marches, nervous waiting, checking weapons and ammunition.
  • During battle: Follow orders, keep your place in line, load and fire as drilled, watch your flag and officers.
  • After battle: Helping the wounded, counting the dead, moving to new positions or back to camp.

Staying alive

  • Stay with your unit: Stragglers are more vulnerable and may be captured or lost.
  • Keep your head: Panic spreads quickly; calm soldiers are more likely to survive and help others.
  • Use cover when allowed: Fences, walls, and terrain can shield you, though tactics often demand standing in the open.
Lines in the smoke

Visibility can be poor, commands hard to hear, and it may be difficult to tell how the battle is going until it’s over.

Camp life

Your real battlefield: the camp

Most of your service will be spent in camp, not in combat. Here you’ll eat, sleep, drill, write letters, and wait. Camps can be temporary “dog tent” sites on campaign or more permanent winter quarters with log huts.

Food and rations

  • Union rations: Hardtack, salted meat, beans, rice, coffee, sugar, sometimes fresh meat and vegetables.
  • Confederate rations: Often less regular and varied—cornmeal, bacon (when available), peas or rice, sometimes foraged food.
  • Supplementing: Foraging, buying from sutlers, or trading with locals or even enemy soldiers when possible.

Passing the time

  • Letters home: Writing and receiving mail is a lifeline to family and friends.
  • Games and music: Cards, dice, checkers, singing, and playing instruments help fight boredom.
  • Religion and reflection: Chaplains hold services; many soldiers turn to faith or journaling.
Winter quarters

In winter camps, soldiers build huts, improve their living spaces, and try to stay warm, fed, and entertained until campaigning resumes.
