Welcome to the army

You’ve traded farm, shop, or city street for a musket and a uniform. Whether you wear Union blue or Confederate gray, your life is about to become a mix of boredom, hardship, fear, and fierce loyalty to the men beside you.

Most of your time will not be spent in glorious charges, but in marching, drilling, waiting, and trying to stay healthy. This guide walks you through what to expect in training, what you’ll wear and carry, how to care for your body, what battle is really like, and how you’ll live in camp.

Training and daily routine

Learning to be a soldier

Training camps turn volunteers into soldiers. Expect long days of drill—learning to march in step, form lines, load and fire your weapon by command, and move as a unit rather than as individuals.

  • Morning: Reveille at dawn, roll call, breakfast, and first drill.
  • Daytime: Company and battalion drill, weapons cleaning, fatigue duties.
  • Evening: Dress parade, mail call, perhaps another drill, then taps.

Discipline and expectations

  • Obedience: Orders from officers and sergeants are to be followed without argument.
  • Punctuality: Being late to roll call or formation can mean extra duty or punishment.
  • Cleanliness of arms: Your musket or rifle must be kept clean and in working order.
Drill, drill, drill

Only a small fraction of your time will be spent in actual combat—most days are repetitive training and camp duties.

Uniforms and equipment

What you’ll wear

  • Union: Dark blue wool coat or sack coat, light blue trousers, forage cap or kepi.
  • Confederate: Gray or “butternut” jackets and trousers, often irregular due to shortages.
  • Undergarments: Cotton or wool shirts and drawers.

What you’ll carry

  • Weapon: Standard infantry arms of the period.
  • Accoutrements: Cartridge box, cap box, belt, bayonet scabbard.
  • Pack: Blanket, extra shirt and socks, tin plate and cup, utensils, personal items.
  • Other: Canteen, haversack for rations.
Blue and gray

Uniforms help identify friend from foe, but in the smoke and confusion of battle, colors and flags are still easy to mistake.

Health and hygiene

Important: In this war, disease kills more soldiers than bullets.

Common dangers

  • Disease: Dysentery, typhoid, pneumonia, measles, malaria.
  • Poor sanitation: Contaminated water and badly placed latrines.
  • Malnutrition: Limited diets weaken the body.

What you can do

  • Wash when you can: Clean hands and face.
  • Protect your feet: Keep them dry and treat blisters early.
  • Use latrines properly: Avoid polluting water sources.
  • Boil water if possible: Reduces disease risk.

Medical care

Army surgeons work under harsh conditions with limited knowledge of infection. Hospitals can save lives, but disease spreads easily.

Field hospitals

Medical care is improving but still primitive.

Battles and combat

What battle is really like

Battle is noise, smoke, confusion, and fear. You’ll stand in long lines, shoulder to shoulder, firing volleys at men doing the same.

  • Before battle: Long marches, nervous waiting.
  • During battle: Follow orders, keep formation.
  • After battle: Aid the wounded, regroup.

Staying alive

  • Stay with your unit: Stragglers are vulnerable.
  • Keep your head: Panic spreads quickly.
  • Use cover when allowed: Terrain can help.
Lines in the smoke

Visibility can be poor, commands hard to hear, and it may be difficult to tell how the battle is going.

Camp life

Your real battlefield: the camp

Most of your service will be spent in camp. Camps can be temporary tent sites or more permanent winter quarters.

Food and rations

  • Union rations: Hardtack, salted meat, beans, rice, coffee.
  • Confederate rations: Cornmeal, bacon, peas or rice.
  • Supplementing: Foraging, buying from sutlers.

Passing the time

  • Letters home: A lifeline to family.
  • Games and music: Cards, dice, singing.
  • Religion and reflection: Chaplains hold services.
Winter quarters

Soldiers build huts, improve their living spaces, and try to stay warm.
